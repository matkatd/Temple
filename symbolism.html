Temples: The Mountain of the Lord

Symbolism and the Temple

“The house of the Lord, bathed in light, standing out in the darkness, becomes symbolic of the power and the inspiration of the gospel of Jesus Christ standing as a beacon in a world that sinks ever further into spiritual darkness”
-President Boyd K. Packer

Why Symbolism?

The Lord loves symbolism. The idea of using one thing to remind or teach us of a deeper truth has been used by the Lord since Adam and Eve. When they sacrificed the firstlings of their flocks, they were taught that it was a symbol of the Atonement of Christ. Symbolism allows the Lord to teach us very personally. Because symbols can have many different meanings, the Lord can reveal what is relevant to us at the moment we need it. Ultimately, each symbol is the temple points us to Christ. Elder David E. Sorensen taught that temples “symbolically and literally remind us and teach us about Christ and His Father. … Our temple worship today includes many symbolic references to Christ, from the spires on the outside that point our minds heavenward, to the white clothing we wear inside the temple.”

Temples as "Templates"

It is interesting that the English words “temple” and “template” both come from the same latin root, “templum.” With this in mind, we can look at the temple as a “template” for the Plan of Salvation. Author David J. Ridges wrote, “The presentation of the endowment in the temple culminates in the symbolic bestowal of eternal life in the celestial kingdom upon the worthy participants. The temple endowment, then, is among other things a presentation, a representation, and a symbol.” Each ordinance in the temple is symbolic of our eternal progression into the presence of God.

Architectural Symbolism

I believe that the Lord almost never does anything for a singular reason. He always has the entire eternal picture in mind, and thus can fulfill many purposes with one action. Such is the case with the physical structure of temples. Not only are temples beautiful structures, but they are object lessons as well. The beauty reminds us of the kingdom of heaven. The spires remind us to look up to God. The angel that adorns many temples reminds us that the gospel has been restored in the last days, and they when we exit the temple we are prepared to join in proclaiming the good news. The physical placement of various rooms remind us of our progression in this life as we move towards our Heavenly Father. The twelve oxen in the baptismal font represent the twelve tribes of Israel, and that when we enter into the baptismal covenant we become members of the house of Israel.
